How the built environment affects us

How buildings are built, where they are built, and how the streets around them are designed directly affect your health.

And those things are dictated by our laws and regulations

How do buildings and streets affect our health?

Walkability

Just because a place has sidewalks doesn't mean people will want to walk there

Walkability refers to places that are designed to encourage walking by providing a safe and enjoyable experience.

What Walkability Isn't

What Walkability Is

So what makes a place Walkable?

Sidewalks that are wide and clear of obstructions

Narrow buildings - variety

Shade - street trees, buildings, awnings

Safety - slow cars, separated from traffic

Walkability and Physical Health

Communities where walking is a viable form of transportation are slimmer and have lower health costs

When streets are designed to focus on cars, it is at the expense of bike and pedestrian safety

Today, most air pollution comes from driving, not factories

Mental Health and Community

Car-centric suburbs are socially isolating

Livable Streets - Donald Appleyard

Late 1960's study that compared 3 residential streets in San Francisco

All streets were similar except for the amount of traffic.

2000 vehicles per day was considered Light Traffic, 8000 Medium traffic, and 16000 Heavy Traffic

How does zoning enforce inequality and car dependence?

Zoning can make it either illegal or prohibitively expensive to build housing that is inherently more affordable

These types of housing are sometimes referred to as Missing Middle Housing on account of how rarely they are built today compared to the past

Single family zoning is an apartment ban

When you only allow for the most expensive type of housing you exclude those that can't afford it.

Lexington's existing housing stock is ~63% single family detached housing, with an extra ~6% single family attached (townhomes)

So what can we doing to fix it?

Affordability

Inclusive Zoning

This is when cities give developers allowances (let them build taller, let them build less parking) if the developer agrees to rent out a percentage of new units at below market rates.

Effectiveness is still debated

Increase the amount and variety of housing

Change the regulations to make it easier to build more types of housing

Want to build a single family house?

Cool, just need one off street parking spot.

Want to build a duplex?

That needs 4 off street parking spaces!

Remove some of the "thousand cuts" that kill projects

  • Require less parking (we are over parked as is)
  • Relax setback requirements
  • Allow duplexes (or even tri-plexes) by right

Allow more housing types: ADUs

Accessory Dwelling Unit

Lexington is on its way to re-legalize ADUs.

Proposed ADU Ordinance

  • No lot size limit
  • No required parking
  • 800sqft max or 50% of primary house
  • Owner occupancy only required for short term rentals

But it's also important that we try to build in walkability

Lexington is trying to address this with it's new Comprehensive Plan and Placebuilder guidelines
